# JoyMaze Content Archetypes — Story-First Framework
Last updated: 2026-03-27

> This document defines the 8 story archetypes that drive all JoyMaze content.
> Every image post and video is built from one of these archetypes.
> Read alongside config/writing-style.md (the execution guide) and ACTIVE_SPRINT.md (current priorities).

---

## Core Philosophy

**Story is the lifeblood.** People share what they feel, not what they're sold.
80% of content exists purely to be liked, saved, and shared — building the audience.
20% weaves in marketing, and it works *because* the 80% earned the trust.

A good story post builds more brand equity than 10 product posts.
The goal is not conversion per post. It is conversion per relationship.

## CTA Placement Rules

- **Profile bio:** "Free on iOS & Android → joymaze.com"
- **First pinned comment:** on every Archetype 7 post and Marketing Anchor post
- **In-post CTA:** only on Marketing Anchor posts (1/day), and only as a soft invitation — never a command
- **Never:** CTAs inside Archetypes 1–6 posts or Archetype 8 videos. The story IS the work.

**The Hormozi principle:** If the content is good and entertaining, it doesn't need a CTA in the post itself.
Great content earns the follow. The follow earns the click. The click earns the download.

## The 3-Beat Story Structure

Every story archetype follows this spine:

```
BEAT 1 — SCENE DROP
  One vivid, specific moment. No setup. Drop into the middle of it.
  Sensory and concrete: time of day, what the child was doing, the feeling in the room.

BEAT 2 — EMOTIONAL TENSION
  The friction or feeling that every parent recognizes.
  Name it without explaining it. They already know this feeling.

BEAT 3 — THE SHIFT
  Something changes. The room goes quiet. The child leans in. The parent exhales.
  In pure story posts: the shift is the end. No product.
  In subtle marketing posts: JoyMaze is present in the shift — as the setting, not the hero.
```

## ARCHETYPE 1 — The Restless Afternoon

**Emotional core:** Overwhelm → Calm
**JoyMaze angle:** The moment a restless child finds their anchor
**Marketing level:** None — pure story

**3-beat structure:**
- Scene: A specific afternoon. Time of day, weather optional. Child is restless, bouncing, nothing sticking.
- Tension: Parent has tried everything. The afternoon feels heavy.
- Shift: Something lands. The room changes. The quiet arrives.

**What NOT to do:** Don't name JoyMaze. Don't mention the app. Don't explain why the shift happened. Let it be mystery.

**Example executions:**

> It was 3:47pm on a Tuesday.
> She'd been restless since school — tablet, snacks, the couch, the floor.
> Then she found the coloring page she'd printed last week.
> Twenty minutes later, the whole room had changed.

---

> Rainy afternoon. Three failed activities.
> He sat there, cross-legged, pencil in hand, and stared at the maze like it owed him something.
> Then he found the path.
> And the afternoon found its shape.

---

> Nothing was landing. She was tired but not sleepy.
> I almost gave up on the afternoon.
> Then she got quiet in the way that means she's found something.
> I didn't interrupt it.

## ARCHETYPE 2 — The Discovery

**Emotional core:** Skepticism → Surprise → Delight
**JoyMaze angle:** The moment a parent discovers something works unexpectedly well
**Marketing level:** None — pure story

**3-beat structure:**
- Scene: Parent tries something new (or lets child try something). Low expectations.
- Tension: The doubt. "This probably won't stick." The familiar feeling of trying and failing.
- Shift: It works. Something unexpected happens. The child stays.

**What NOT to do:** Don't name the product. Don't tell them what to discover. Don't over-explain the result.

**Example executions:**

> I didn't think it would hold her attention for more than five minutes.
> She'd burned through four other things that afternoon.
> But she was still there an hour later.
> Pencil in hand. Leaning in.
> I was the skeptic. She proved me wrong.

---

> He asked for it again the next morning.
> That surprised me more than anything.
> He never asks for things twice.

---

> I gave it to her as a last resort.
> By the time dinner was ready, she hadn't moved.
> That's when I stopped calling it a last resort.

## ARCHETYPE 3 — Childhood Nostalgia

**Emotional core:** Longing → Reconnection → Warmth
**JoyMaze angle:** The timeless joy of coloring books, mazes, puzzles — what parents remember
**Marketing level:** None — pure story

**3-beat structure:**
- Scene: A specific childhood memory. A Saturday morning. A grandmother's house. The smell of crayons.
- Tension: That feeling is gone from modern life. Everything is faster, louder, more distracted.
- Shift: A child today finds that same stillness. The feeling didn't disappear. It was waiting.

**What NOT to do:** Don't make it about the app. This is about the emotion. Let the reader's memory do the work.

**Example executions:**

> Remember when a box of crayons felt like the whole world?
> The smell of them. The sound of choosing.
> That feeling didn't go anywhere.
> It's just waiting in the right moment.

---

> Saturday mornings used to feel different.
> Slower. Quieter. A maze book and nowhere to be.
> My kid had that moment yesterday.
> In a different form. The same feeling.

---

> My grandmother had a drawer full of activity books.
> No screens. Just puzzles and colored pencils and hours that went soft.
> I didn't know I was building memories.
> I think my kid is building some right now.

## ARCHETYPE 4 — The Quiet Room

**Emotional core:** Guilt → Relief → Permission
**JoyMaze angle:** The exhale parents get when screen time finally feels okay
**Marketing level:** None — pure story

**3-beat structure:**
- Scene: The parent. What they were doing. The background noise.
- Tension: The constant hum of screen-time guilt. The awareness that something feels off.
- Shift: The room goes quiet in the right way. Not silence — focus. The parent exhales.

**What NOT to do:** Don't moralize. Don't explain what made it okay. Don't pitch anything. Just describe the feeling.

**Example executions:**

> All she wanted was twenty minutes.
> Not distracted — actually peaceful.
> The kind of quiet where you can hear yourself think.
> She got it today.
> So did her daughter.

---

> I used to hear the noise of the tablet from the other room and feel it.
> That low-level guilt. The "I should be doing something better" feeling.
> Yesterday afternoon, the room was quiet.
> And the guilt was gone.
> Just gone.

---

> There's a difference between a child being occupied and a child being absorbed.
> I felt it today.
> I didn't know I'd been waiting for it.

## ARCHETYPE 5 — The Curious Child

**Emotional core:** Wonder → Engagement → Pride
**JoyMaze angle:** A child solving something, creating something, asking to come back
**Marketing level:** None — pure story

**3-beat structure:**
- Scene: The child, up close. What their hands are doing. The expression on their face.
- Tension: The moment before they solve it — the concentration, the almost-there.
- Shift: They get it. Or they make something. The pride that follows — theirs and the parent's.

**What NOT to do:** Don't make the parent the hero. The child is the hero. Don't explain what they learned. Show the moment.

**Example executions:**

> He stared at it for four minutes.
> I watched him from across the room — not interrupting.
> His lips moved a little. His pencil moved.
> Then: "I got it."
> He said it to himself, not to me.
> That part was the best part.

---

> She colored outside the lines on purpose.
> Then she colored inside them on purpose.
> Then she held it up and showed me like it was evidence.
> "I made this different," she said.
> She had.

---

> He asked to do the hard one.
> Not the easy one. The hard one.
> I didn't have to teach him that.
> He just decided he wanted the challenge.
> That was all him.

## ARCHETYPE 6 — Before I Knew This

**Emotional core:** Frustration → Clarity → Relief
**JoyMaze angle:** The parent who found a better way — discovery arc
**Marketing level:** None — pure story

**3-beat structure:**
- Scene: A before-state. What wasn't working. The search.
- Tension: The exhaustion of trying things and not finding what sticks.
- Shift: Something shifted. Not explained as a product pitch — just the feeling of finally.

**What NOT to do:** Don't name a product. Don't give a tutorial. Don't turn it into a recommendation. The story ends at the shift.

**Example executions:**

> For a long time, I felt like I was doing it wrong.
> Every app felt like a compromise.
> Occupying without engaging. Passing time without building anything.
> Then something shifted.
> I didn't have to feel that way anymore.

---

> I spent a lot of afternoons trying to find the thing that would hold her attention without making me feel guilty.
> Not a screen. Not a toy. Something in between.
> I stopped searching when I found it.
> I wish I'd found it sooner.

---

> The research kept saying the same thing: less screen time.
> But that wasn't actually the problem.
> The problem was the kind of screen time.
> Once I understood that, everything got easier.

## ARCHETYPE 7 — The Parent Who Chose

**Emotional core:** Identity → Confidence → Pride
**JoyMaze angle:** JoyMaze appears naturally as the setting — not the hero, just present
**Marketing level:** Subtle — product mentioned once, as context, never as a pitch

**3-beat structure:**
- Scene: A parent making a deliberate choice. The moment of choosing something different.
- Tension: The awareness that most choices are automatic. This one was intentional.
- Shift: The result. The child absorbed. The parent proud. JoyMaze is in the scene but not the point.

**What NOT to do:** Don't let JoyMaze be the hero. The parent is the hero. JoyMaze is the setting. One mention maximum.

**Example executions:**

> She didn't just hand her daughter a tablet.
> She opened JoyMaze, chose a coloring page together, and left her to it.
> Twenty minutes later, she could hear the pencil on the paper from the kitchen.
> She made that choice.
> That's the part that mattered.

---

> He could have put on a show.
> He didn't.
> He opened the maze app instead — the one his daughter always asked for.
> She didn't ask to stop for an hour.
> That was his choice.
> That was his hour too.

---

> Some parents Google "is screen time bad for kids" at 11pm.
> She used to.
> Now she just knows what she's handing them.
> The guilt left when the choice got clearer.

## ARCHETYPE 8 — Kids Story (Video)

**Emotional core:** Wonder → Adventure → Delight
**JoyMaze angle:** Purely the world of kids creativity — animals, curiosity, problem-solving, friendship
**Marketing level:** None — zero product mention, zero CTA

**Purpose:** Pure reach. Shares. Saves. New followers who discover JoyMaze through the story.
The story builds the audience. The audience converts through other content.

**Format:**
- 45–60 seconds (scalable to 3–5 minutes later)
- 5–8 AI-generated images + text overlays + soft background music
- Joyo narrates (as guide/narrator, appears at start and end — "Joyo's Story Corner")
- Main characters: animals, vehicles, fantastical creatures — not JoyMaze characters
- Recurring series mechanic: "Joyo's Story Corner — Episode [N]"

**3-beat story structure:**
- ACT 1 (15s): Introduce the character. Their world. Their problem or wish.
- ACT 2 (30s): The adventure or challenge. Something goes wrong. Something must be solved.
- ACT 3 (15s): Resolution. A small but real emotional payoff. The lesson lives in the feeling, not a moral speech.

**Story themes that travel:**
- A small animal solves a big problem through cleverness
- A lost creature finds their way home
- Two very different characters become unexpected friends
- A curious child discovers something magical in an ordinary place
- An animal who feels left out finds what makes them special

**What NOT to do:** No product mentions. No CTAs. No "download JoyMaze." No brand overlays inside the story. Joyo only bookends — does not appear mid-story unless Joyo IS the main character.

**Example episode outlines:**

**Episode 1: The Fox and the Frozen River**
> A small fox needs to cross a frozen river to bring food to her family before the storm arrives.
> The ice is thin. Every path she tries breaks.
> She watches how the birds land — light and quick.
> She learns to move like them.
> She crosses.
> Not by being bigger. By paying attention.

---

**Episode 2: The Elephant Who Forgot**
> An elephant can't remember where he buried his favorite thing.
> Every animal in the jungle offers to help him look.
> They search all morning.
> It wasn't the thing that mattered.
> It was the morning they spent looking for it together.

---

**Episode 3: The Little Lighthouse**
> A small lighthouse on a rocky shore hasn't been lit in years.
> A child discovers it during a storm.
> She climbs it. She lights it.
> Seven boats find their way home that night.
> She never knew their names. They never knew hers.
> But the light was hers.

## ARCHETYPE 9 — ASMR Activity Video

**Emotional core:** Curiosity → Calm → Satisfaction
**JoyMaze angle:** The satisfying process of a kids activity unfolding step by step
**Marketing level:** None — minimal branding, zero CTA

**Purpose:** Reach through the ASMR/satisfying content algorithm. These videos perform on TikTok and Instagram Reels because they trigger the "oddly satisfying" engagement loop. Parents watch for calm, kids watch for the visual progression. Pure reach play — no selling.

**Format:**
- 30–50 seconds
- 4–6 AI-generated progression images (empty → 25% → 50% → 75% → complete)
- NO voiceover, NO text overlays on slides
- Optional ambient music at very low volume (15% mix)
- Slow Ken Burns effects (6% zoom), 1-second crossfade transitions
- Minimal intro/outro (JoyMaze name only, no CTA, no mascot)
- Output: 1080x1920 (9:16) for TikTok/Reels/Shorts

**Content types that work:**
- Coloring page progression (most satisfying — blank page to finished art)
- Maze being solved step by step (path filling in)
- Dot-to-dot connecting progressively (shape emerging)
- Tracing activity being completed (lines forming)
- Drawing activity (sketch → outline → color → finished)

**3-beat structure (visual only — no narration):**
- OPENING (5s): The blank/empty activity. Clean. Potential.
- PROGRESSION (25-35s): Each slide shows more completion. The transformation is the story.
- FINISHED PIECE (8s): The completed activity. Hold on it. Let them admire it.

**What NOT to do:** No voiceover. No text overlays on slides. No CTAs. No product mentions. No "download JoyMaze." No challenge language. No exclamation marks in captions. The calm IS the content.

**Example activity progressions:**

**ASMR Coloring — Ocean Scene:**
> Slide 1: Empty ocean coloring page (line art only)
> Slide 2: Water colored in soft blue
> Slide 3: Fish and coral added with warm colors
> Slide 4: Details filled — shells, bubbles, seaweed
> Slide 5: Finished piece — vibrant ocean scene

**ASMR Maze — Forest Path:**
> Slide 1: Empty forest maze with start/end marked
> Slide 2: Path traced 25% through the trees
> Slide 3: Path at halfway point, wrong turns visible
> Slide 4: Path nearly complete
> Slide 5: Solved maze — full path highlighted

**ASMR Tracing — Butterfly:**
> Slide 1: Dotted butterfly outline
> Slide 2: Wings traced on one side
> Slide 3: Full outline traced
> Slide 4: Colors being added
> Slide 5: Finished butterfly with all details

## Activity Post Archetypes (A1–A5)

Activity posts are **actual puzzles and printable activities** — not lifestyle photos. People save, share, and print these. They drive engagement through interaction, not emotion.

Every activity image gets a **branded JoyMaze watermark** (logo + joymaze.com). The caption includes a soft brand mention. The activity itself is free value.

### A1 — Maze Puzzle

**Purpose:** Problem-solving challenge. Visual engagement. High save rate.
**What to generate:** An actual maze image — clear paths, dead ends, single solution. Animal/adventure themed for kids.
**Difficulty levels:** Easy (5-6 age, few dead ends), Medium (6-7 age), Hard (7-8 age, complex paths)
**Visual rules:**
- Clean lines, high contrast (works when printed)
- Theme the maze: animal shape, castle, underwater, space
- Start and end clearly marked
- DO NOT show the solution
- Leave bottom 10% clear for JoyMaze watermark
**Hook style:** "Can your kid find the path?" / "This maze stumped 3 out of 4 kids"
**Skill framing:** Problem-solving, spatial reasoning, focus

### A2 — Word Search

**Purpose:** Vocabulary + pattern recognition. Parents love these for "educational" feel.
**What to generate:** A word search grid with 6-10 hidden words. Theme: animals, food, colors, seasons, etc.
**Difficulty levels:** Easy (5-6 words, large grid), Medium (7-8 words), Hard (9-10 words, diagonal)
**Visual rules:**
- Clear, readable letter grid (large font)
- Word list shown beside or below grid
- Colorful header with theme title
- DO NOT circle or highlight any words (let them find them)
- Leave bottom 10% clear for watermark
**Hook style:** "Find all 8 words!" / "How fast can they spot them all?"
**Skill framing:** Vocabulary, letter recognition, focus, scanning

### A3 — Matching / Spot-the-Difference

**Purpose:** Memory and observation. Great for younger kids (4-6).
**What to generate:** Either find-the-matching-pair OR spot-the-difference between two images.
**Difficulty levels:** Easy (4 pairs, obvious), Medium (6 pairs), Hard (spot-the-difference with subtle changes)
**Visual rules:**
- Bright, colorful illustrations
- Objects clearly separated with space between
- For spot-the-difference: two images side by side, 3-5 differences
- Leave bottom 10% clear for watermark
**Hook style:** "Which two are exactly the same?" / "Can you spot all 5 differences?"
**Skill framing:** Observation, memory, attention to detail

### A4 — Tracing / Drawing Activity

**Purpose:** Fine motor skills. Pre-writing practice. Very popular with parents of younger kids.
**What to generate:** Dotted-line paths, letter tracing, shape tracing, or guided drawing steps.
**Difficulty levels:** Easy (simple curves), Medium (letters/numbers), Hard (complex shapes)
**Visual rules:**
- Dotted or dashed lines clearly visible
- Guiding arrows showing direction
- Fun theme: "Help the bunny get home" with dotted path
- Thick lines that work when printed
- Leave bottom 10% clear for watermark
**Hook style:** "Trace the path to help Joyo!" / "Perfect practice for little hands"
**Skill framing:** Fine motor skills, hand-eye coordination, pre-writing

### A5 — Quiz / Visual Puzzle

**Purpose:** Quick engagement. Comments and shares from parents comparing answers.
**What to generate:** Odd-one-out, counting challenge, pattern completion, "which shadow matches?", visual riddles.
**Difficulty levels:** Easy (obvious odd-one-out), Medium (count + compare), Hard (visual riddle)
**Visual rules:**
- Eye-catching, colorful
- Question/challenge text embedded in image
- Answer NOT shown (reveal in comments or next post)
- Works as a standalone — no context needed
- Leave bottom 10% clear for watermark
**Hook style:** "How many can YOU spot?" / "Only 1 in 5 kids get this right" / "What comes next?"
**Skill framing:** Logic, counting, pattern recognition, critical thinking

### Why this mix replaces story archetypes

The 8-archetype story framework required custom Joyo character scenes for every post — operationally expensive and potentially triggering platform spam detection (formulaic branded content). The new mix:
- Uses **activity images as the hero** — the actual printable is the product signal
- Pulls **trend intelligence weekly** (Monday refresh) into the Fact-Card and challenge themes
- Eliminates custom character art for slots 1-5 — simpler to generate, more Pinterest-native
- Every slot is **activity-adjacent** — direct path to download / print

### Posting Architecture — Hourly Drip

All creative posts (slots 1-10) are now assigned a `scheduledHour` on import.
The Task Scheduler runs `post-content.mjs --scheduled --limit 2` every hour.
Posts drip naturally throughout the day — no bulk firing.

```
Task Scheduler jobs (Windows):
  post-x-scheduled.mjs     → every hour  → X text posts (4/day, 7am-7pm)
  post-content.mjs --scheduled → every hour → creative posts (10/day, 6am-9pm)
```
